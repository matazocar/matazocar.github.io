So this is actually a second part to the previous post where I was counting triangles.

As I was talking with my advisor, he taught me that, in fact, this result is a well known quantity and it's called "Goodman counting" due to Adolph Winkler Goodman, who was a prolific mathematician from the United States with several contributions to number theory, graph theory and theory of univalent functions. In particular, on his paper ["On sets of acquaintances and strangers at any party"](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2310464?seq=1) he proposes an alternative proof for $R(3,3)=6$. Moreover, he shows that there exists at least two monochromatic triangles within any $2$-edge-colouring of the complete graph in $6$ vertices.

With this new information, I could find some [notes](https://ywigderson.math.ethz.ch/math/static/RamseyMultiplicity-Stanford.pdf) from Yuval Wigderson which expand a little bit more onto the topic of Ramsey multiplicity.
